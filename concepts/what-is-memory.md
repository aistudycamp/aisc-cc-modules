# Memory

## What is it?

Imagine you have a brilliant assistant, but every morning they wake up with total amnesia. You'd have to re-explain who you are, what your project is, and what you decided yesterday — every single day. That's what working with AI normally feels like. Close the chat, open a new one, and everything is gone.

Memory fixes that. It's like a **notebook Claude keeps between conversations**. When Claude learns something important during a session — your preferences, a decision you made, context about your project — it can write that down. The next time you start a conversation, Claude checks its notebook and picks up where you left off.

## How is this different from CLAUDE.md?

Good question — they work together, but they're different:
- **CLAUDE.md** (Module 1) is instructions *you* write to shape Claude's behavior. You are the author.
- **Memory** is context *Claude* learns and saves during conversations. Claude is the author (with your permission).

Think of it this way: CLAUDE.md is like the employee handbook you write before someone starts. Memory is the personal notes they take on the job as they learn your preferences.

## Why does it matter?

Memory means you stop repeating yourself. Instead of re-explaining your project, your preferences, or past decisions every session, Claude remembers them. This makes each conversation faster and more useful than the last.

## How does it work?

- During a conversation, when Claude notices something worth remembering (a preference, a decision, a fact about your work), it saves it as a short, clear note
- These notes are stored as small text files on your computer — you can open them in any text editor, and they stay on your machine (they're not sent anywhere)
- When a new session starts, Claude checks for relevant memories and naturally incorporates them
- You're always in control — you can review, edit, or delete any memory anytime

The more you use Claude, the more helpful it becomes. Day 1, Claude knows nothing about you. A month in, Claude knows your preferences, your project context, and your working style — all from the notes it's built up over time.

## Real-world examples

- **Remembering preferences:** "Nicole prefers bullet points over long paragraphs" carries across every future session
- **Tracking project context:** "We decided to use blue as the primary brand color at the March 15 meeting" stays available without re-explaining
- **Recalling past decisions:** "We chose Mailchimp over ConvertKit for the newsletter — here's why" so you don't revisit settled debates
- **Keeping a running log:** "Sprint 1 focused on the landing page; Sprint 2 is tackling the checkout flow" so Claude always knows where the project stands

## Quick check

You told Claude last week that your manager's name is Jordan and your team communicates on Slack. Today you ask Claude to draft a project update. How might memory change Claude's response compared to an AI without memory?

*(Claude might suggest posting the update in your team's Slack channel and tagging Jordan — without you having to explain any of that context again. That's memory in action.)*
